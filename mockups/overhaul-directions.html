GL Universe · Total Overhaul · Concept Round 01

Four Directions

Not a reskin — four structurally different answers to "what is the GL Universe HUD?". Each direction redesigns the key element (the time bar) plus a button, an initiative card and the round numeral in its own voice, with its own shading physics and motion signature. Click each stage to fire that direction's time-advance animation. Pick one — or tell me which parts of which to splice.

A

RACKLINE — Modular Instrument Rail

The bar stops being one slab. It shatters into discrete instrument modules docked on a powered rail — each system (weather, date, watch, clock, mission) is its own physical block with its own height, connector pin and bracket. Add a module, it docks; remove one, the rack closes up. Data is physical: you can watch a pulse travel the rail and kick each module as it passes.

B

CASTLIGHT — Holographic Projection

Kill the container entirely. The HUD is a borderless light construct standing on a single baseline rule — no panel, no background, just type, ticks and faint projection cones. Edges fringe with CMYK chromatic aberration (the print-press motif turned into optics), a scan beam patrols the construct, motes drift, and the whole thing breathes. State changes de-rez and reassemble like a hologram losing sync for a frame.

C

BASALT — Obsidian Monolith

Maximum physicality. One thick, slanted slab of volcanic glass — the whole bar is an Endfield diagonal — with milled notches on the top edge, a molten amber inlay running its length, and content that reads as embedded at different depths inside the glass: a blurred numeral stratum drifts behind the sharp dial layer with true cursor parallax. A specular streak tracks your pointer across the polished surface; the slab casts a floor reflection. Time advances by core ignition — an amber bloom from inside the stone.

D

MANIFEST — Print Deck

Go all-in on Endfield's print-press soul. The HUD is a dark glass clipboard with physical paper chips clipped to it — off-white labels with punch-holes and hard offset shadows, one opaque safety-yellow block for the mission, a barcode, CMYK chips, slight rotations like real clipped notes. Light text on dark glass for ambient data; black-on-paper for what the table must read. Time advances with a rubber-stamp slam and a sheet wipe.
